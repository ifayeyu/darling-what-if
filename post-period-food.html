What to Eat During Your Period —
And What's Making Everything Worse

May 19, 2026
By Gia
7 min read

Your body isn't being dramatic. It's asking for something specific — and it matters what you reach for.

There's a particular kind of cruelty to period cravings. You feel awful, so you want the chip bag, the chocolate biscuit, the second coffee. And for about twenty minutes, you feel slightly less awful — and then somehow, inexplicably, worse. The bloating tightens. The headache returns. The mood dips a little further.

It isn't a lack of willpower. It's that your body, in its pain, is reaching for comfort foods that happen to be exactly the wrong thing. Inflammation feeds on sugar and salt. Cramps are worsened by caffeine. Water retention loves sodium. The foods that feel like relief in the moment are often the ones quietly turning up the volume on everything you're trying to quiet down.

The good news is that this works the other way too. What you eat during your period can genuinely change how you experience it — not eliminate it, but soften it. Your body is working hard. It just needs different support than you might think.

Your period isn't something to push through. It's something to nourish your way through.

What's Making It Louder

Before we get to what helps, it's worth understanding what's making things harder. The main culprits are inflammation and water retention — two processes that your body is already managing during menstruation, and that certain foods push into overdrive.

Salty snacks
Salt encourages your body to hold onto water, which worsens the bloating and heaviness you're already feeling. Crisps, processed crackers, soy sauce-heavy meals — all of it works against you this week.
Sugary sweets and refined carbs
A glucose spike followed by a crash doesn't just affect your energy — it destabilises your mood, worsens irritability, and makes the fatigue that comes with your period much harder to manage.
Caffeine
Caffeine constricts blood vessels, which can intensify cramping. It also interferes with sleep — already harder during your period — and can amplify anxiety and irritability when your hormones are already shifting.
Alcohol
Alcohol is dehydrating, which worsens headaches and fatigue. It also disrupts hormone regulation and disrupts sleep — two things your body genuinely needs during this time.
Red meat and ultra-processed foods
Red meat is high in arachidonic acid, which promotes the kind of inflammatory prostaglandins that make cramps worse. Ultra-processed foods are high in inflammatory additives, refined oils, and salt — not what your body needs when it's already under strain.

None of this means you have to be perfect. If you want the chocolate, have the chocolate — just make it dark. But understanding why these foods make things worse gives you something useful: the ability to choose, rather than just reach.

What you eat this week is a form of care — not restriction.

What Your Body Is Actually Asking For

During menstruation, your body loses iron through bleeding, uses magnesium managing cramps, and works against inflammation throughout. The foods that support you best are the ones that replenish what's being depleted and calm what's being activated. Here's where to start.

  1. Water — more than you think
    Staying well hydrated is one of the most underrated things you can do for your period. It helps reduce the severity of both bleeding and cramping, keeps headaches at bay, and counteracts the water retention that bloating thrives on. Aim for at least 11 to 12 cups a day, and if plain water feels like too much, herbal teas count.
  2. Iron-rich foods — leafy greens, fish, lentils, chicken
    Menstrual bleeding depletes your iron, which is why fatigue, muscle aches, and dizziness tend to peak during your period. Leafy greens like spinach, kale, and Swiss chard are high in iron as well as calcium and zinc. Fish — especially salmon, sardines, and mackerel — adds iron alongside omega-3 fatty acids that actively reduce inflammation and may ease mood swings. Lentils, beans, and chicken are excellent additions, particularly if you follow a plant-based diet.
  3. Ginger and turmeric — your anti-inflammatory allies
    Ginger has genuine anti-inflammatory and muscle-relaxing properties that can ease cramping and nausea. A warm ginger tea in the morning is a simple and deeply comforting way to work it in. Turmeric, with its active compound curcumin, is similarly anti-inflammatory — add it to soups, rice, or a golden milk latte. Keep ginger intake moderate; around 4 grams a day is a sensible ceiling.
  4. Fruit — the sweet answer to sugar cravings
    When the sugar cravings hit — and they will — fruit is the version that actually helps. Berries, watermelon, pears, apples, and grapes are high in water content, rich in micronutrients and antioxidants, and satisfy the craving without the glucose crash that makes your mood plummet an hour later.
  5. Nuts, seeds, and whole grains
    Almonds, walnuts, cashews, and Brazil nuts are nutrient-dense and deeply satisfying — they keep you full and curb the kind of cravings that lead you toward the wrong snacks. Flaxseeds are particularly valuable during your period: rich in fibre and omega-3s, they support digestion and help manage the constipation that is, frustratingly, a common companion to menstruation. Quinoa, as a complete protein and a good source of magnesium and iron, is one of the best whole grains you can reach for this week.
  6. Yogurt, kombucha, and gut-supporting foods
    Probiotic-rich foods like yogurt and kombucha support gut health during your period, when digestive changes are common. They also nourish the vaginal microbiome, which can be more vulnerable to imbalance during menstruation. Choose yogurt without added sugar and kombucha varieties that aren't loaded with sweeteners.
  7. Dark chocolate and peppermint tea — because you deserve something good
    Dark chocolate — at least 80 to 85% cacao — is genuinely beneficial during your period. It's rich in magnesium, which helps with cramping, and studies suggest it can support both cognitive performance and physical recovery during menstruation. A small square or two is not an indulgence; it's a considered choice. Peppermint tea, meanwhile, has been shown to soothe the physical, digestive, and psychological symptoms that cluster around your period. It's the gentlest, most fragrant swap for that second cup of coffee.

A quiet shift, not a strict plan.

You don't need to overhaul your diet or track every meal. Start with one or two changes — swap the crisps for nuts, the coffee for ginger tea, the chocolate bar for a square of dark. Let the rest follow naturally. Your body will notice. And so will you.
