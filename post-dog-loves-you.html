How to Tell If Your Dog Loves You: 10 Signs Backed by Science

July 10, 2026
By Faye Livia
10 min read

You look up — from your phone, your book, your own tangled thoughts — and your dog is already watching you. Soft-eyed. Asking for nothing. And you wonder the way we all secretly wonder: does my dog actually love me? Here’s the tender, science-backed truth.

Every dog person has quietly asked it: how do you tell if your dog loves you, or have you simply trained the world’s most convincing little opportunist? It’s a vulnerable question, and a fair one. So here is the reassuring part, backed by real research: your dog almost certainly loves you — and they’ve been telling you in a dozen small ways you may have been reading as ordinary.

Below are ten science-backed signs your dog loves you, what each one actually means, and — because love is a two-way language — how to gently say it back.

Do Dogs Actually Love Us? What the Science Says

For a long time, “my dog loves me” was treated as sweet human projection. Then the science caught up. In a landmark 2015 study, researchers found that when dogs and their owners simply gaze into each other’s eyes, both experience a rise in oxytocin — the same “love hormone” that bonds a parent and a newborn. Hand-raised wolves don’t do this. It appears to be something dogs evolved specifically to share with us over thousands of years of living side by side.

On top of that chemistry, dogs show what scientists call attachment: they use their favourite person as a “secure base” to explore from and a “safe haven” to return to when frightened — the very same pattern researchers see between infants and their parents. So when we talk about a dog’s love, we’re not being sentimental. We’re describing a measurable, mutual bond.

Your dog’s love isn’t something you have to earn with treats. It’s something they grow toward you, quietly, every single day you make them feel safe.

How to Tell If Your Dog Loves You: 10 Signs

Read these as a pattern, not a checklist — every dog is a little different, and a shy rescue shows love differently than a bouncy puppy. But if several of these feel familiar, you have your answer.

  • They hold soft eye contact. That gentle, relaxed gaze is the oxytocin “love loop” in action. If your dog looks at you with soft eyes and a loose face — not a hard, frozen stare — they’re literally bonding with you.
  • They come to you when they’re scared or unsure. Thunder, a stranger, a vet visit — and they choose you. Seeking you as their safe haven is one of the truest signs of attachment a dog can show.
  • They lean on you. A dog pressing their body or head into your legs is trusting you with their weight. Leaning is a quiet, physical “I’m yours.”
  • They follow you from room to room. If your dog shadows you — the classic “velcro dog” — it usually means you’re their favourite place to be. (More on when following tips into anxiety below.)
  • They bring you their toys. Sharing a treasured toy isn’t always an invitation to play; often it’s a gift and a sign of trust — sharing what matters most with the one who matters most.
  • They greet you like you’ve been gone for years. The wiggle-body, whole-tail, can’t-contain-it welcome after you’ve been out — even for ten minutes — is pure, unfiltered joy at your return.
  • They want to sleep near you (or on you). Sleep is when dogs are most vulnerable. Choosing to rest against you, or at your feet, says they feel completely safe in your presence.
  • They check in with you on walks. That little glance back to make sure you’re still there — “referencing” — means you’re their anchor, even with a whole exciting world to sniff.
  • They give you “puppy-dog eyes.” Research found dogs raise their inner eyebrows — that irresistible pleading look — far more when a human is paying attention. It’s an expression they seem to have evolved just for us.
  • They mirror your calm (and your mood). Dogs “catch” our emotions and even yawn contagiously when we do. A dog who settles when you settle, and softens when you’re sad, is deeply tuned to you.
A wag is worth reading closely

Not every tail wag means the same thing. Researchers have found that dogs wag slightly more to the right when they see something they love (like their owner) and more to the left when they’re unsure. A loose, whole-body, sweeping wag is happy; a high, stiff, fast wag can mean arousal or tension. Always read the tail together with the ears, eyes and body.

Signs Your Dog Feels Safe and Happy With You

Love and safety are the same thing to a dog. If you want to read your dog’s body language, these are the signs of a relaxed, happy, securely-attached dog:

  • A loose, wiggly body rather than a stiff, still one.
  • Soft eyes and a relaxed, slightly open mouth — the doggy equivalent of an easy smile.
  • Ears in a natural, neutral position, not pinned back or rigidly forward.
  • A play bow — front down, back end up — inviting you into the fun.
  • Rolling over for a belly rub, which means they trust you with their most vulnerable spot.
  • A deep sigh as they flop down beside you — the sound of a nervous system letting go.

How to Love Your Dog Back (in a Language They Understand)

Here’s the beautiful part: you can answer all of this. To show your dog you love them in a way they actually feel:

  • Offer soft eye contact and slow blinks. Meet their gaze gently — you’re feeding the same oxytocin loop right back to them.
  • Let them sniff on walks. A “sniffari” is how dogs read the world. Letting them linger is a real act of love, not a delay.
  • Keep their days predictable. Consistent meals, walks and bedtimes tell a dog: you are safe, you are cared for, nothing here will surprise you.
  • Touch them where they like it. Most dogs prefer a chest or shoulder scratch to a pat on the head. Watch what makes them lean in.
  • Train with kindness and rewards. Positive reinforcement builds trust; it’s teaching wrapped in reassurance.
  • Give them your unhurried presence. Not multitasking, not scrolling — just being with them. Presence is the whole love language.

When It Might Be More Than Love

A dog who follows you everywhere is usually just devoted. But it’s worth gently paying attention if that closeness tips into distress: constant shadowing paired with whining, pacing, panting, drooling or destruction when left alone can be a sign of separation anxiety rather than affection, and it’s something a vet or a certified behaviourist can genuinely help with. Likewise, a normally independent dog who suddenly becomes clingy can sometimes be telling you they’re unwell or in pain. Love is the usual answer — but a sudden change in behaviour always deserves a closer, caring look.

To your dog, you are the whole weather of their world.

You are their morning, their safety, their favourite sound on the stairs. The next time your dog looks up at you for no reason at all, you’ll know it isn’t nothing — it’s a small, wordless way of saying the thing you were hoping for all along. Look back softly. They’ll understand.
